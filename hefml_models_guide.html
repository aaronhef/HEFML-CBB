HefML Models Guide

Hoops spreads · Football weeks · How to read them

This page explains how to read the two HefML models: the basketball spread & win-probability model and the college football weekly model.

Both are built to answer two questions:

  • Who is more likely to win? (moneyline / win probability)
  • What margin is most likely? (spread, plus a low–mid–high range)

Below you’ll see how to interpret the ovals, colors, and ranges, plus a couple of simple examples so you can sanity-check real slates before you bet.

Model 1 · College Basketball

Hoops: Win Probability & Spread Distribution

For each game, the HefML Hoops model shows two things:

  • Home win probability — the green/red oval in the upper right. If it’s above 50%, the home team is the favorite. If it drops below 50%, the away team is actually favored.
  • Spread distribution — the three ovals on the bottom, which describe the range of plausible final margins if the game were played 100 times.

The spreads are always in home – away format:

  • Positive spread → home is favored by that many points.
  • Negative spread → away is favored by that many points.

Reading the three spread ovals

The spread band is meant to feel like the “S-curve” of win probability, just collapsed down to three key points:

  • Low — roughly the 10th percentile outcome.
  • Pred — the 50th percentile / median; the margin you’d expect most often.
  • High — roughly the 90th percentile outcome.

Example: A strong home favorite

UC Irvine vs West Georgia
Home favored in every scenario.
Home win probability 88.4%
Spread (home − away)
Low: +3.1 Pred: +11.3 High: +29.1

In this game, the model said something like:

  • Low: home wins by about 3 if a lot goes wrong.
  • Pred: home wins by about 11 most of the time.
  • High: home wins by nearly 30 if everything clicks.

When the actual final lands between Pred and High (like a 20–25 point win), it’s basically “things went well for the favorite,” but not an outrageous outlier.

When spread and win probability disagree

By default, if the home win probability is above 50%, the home spread should be positive (home favored). Occasionally, though, you’ll see weird cases where:

  • The model leans one way on the spread but the moneyline is closer to a coin flip.
  • The public market has a team favored to win but effectively a dog to cover.

Those “spread vs win prob” divergences are rare in the model, but you do see versions of that in actual public lines. When you do, it’s a sign to slow down and understand why — pace, injuries, or garbage-time risk.

How to actually use this to bet

1 · Side selection
Compare the model’s Pred spread to the public line. If the model thinks the favorite should be -14.5 and the book is hanging -9.5, that’s a strong edge on the favorite. If the model says -6 and the book is -12, that’s value on the dog.
2 · Sanity check with Low/High
If the public line sits outside the Low/High band, the market is basically saying “we think the extreme outcome is the baseline.” That’s often worth a bet — or at least a much closer look.
Model 2 · College Football

Football: Weekly HefML Portal

The HefML football model is laid out as a weekly portal: each week’s slate shows the home and away teams, the model spread, and the win probability, all skinned in the same oval style as Hoops.

Under the hood, it works similarly:

  • Each game gets a home win probability (moneyline view).
  • The model produces an implied point spread for the matchup.
  • Games are organized by week and by team schedule so you can scan quickly.

Sample football tile

Indiana at Ohio State
Example Week · Road test for the visitor.
Home win probability 63.0%
Model spread (home) OSU -4.2

Here the model is saying: Ohio State is a moderate favorite to win outright, and should be around a 4–5 point favorite on the spread. If the public line is OSU -7, the value is probably on Indiana +7. If the public line is OSU -2.5, the value leans toward laying the small number with the home favorite.

Differences vs Hoops

  • Football games have fewer possessions, so a couple of fluky drives can swing the final margin more than in basketball.
  • The football model leans on power ratings, tempo, returning production, and situational factors, not just raw scoring margins.
  • You’ll often use the football portal to find a short list of games where the model and Vegas are off by 2–3 points or more, then layer in injury / coaching news before betting.
3 · Weekly scan
Use the portal to scan each week for games where the model spread is off from the public number by 2+ points. Those go into the “shortlist” for real bets.
4 · Parlay building
For parlays, you can combine a couple of higher-confidence favorites (big win probabilities and model spreads close to the public line) with one or two dogs where the model shows a clear edge vs Vegas.

Putting it together

In both models, the philosophy is the same:

  • Start with win probability to understand who should win and how often.
  • Use the spread and the low / pred / high band to see how the game usually plays out on the scoreboard.
  • Compare that to the public line. The bigger the gap, the more interesting the bet.

The goal isn’t to predict every game perfectly. It’s to consistently find spots where the model’s picture of the matchup is meaningfully different from the market, and then let the edges add up over time.
